# Week 5 — Challenges

1. **[Challenge 1 — ARIA-rich listbox](./challenge-01-aria-rich-listbox.md)** — build a fully accessible custom listbox following the WAI-ARIA Authoring Practices end-to-end, including roving tabindex, type-ahead, and screen-reader testing. (~140 min)

Challenges are optional but transformational. Skip them and you will know the DOM and events well. Do them and the components you ship will be the kind a screen-reader user actually uses without friction.

## How challenges differ from exercises

- **Exercises** are small drills with a known answer. You can grade yourself by counting `✓` lines or by clicking through with the mouse.
- **Challenges** are larger problems that require judgment. There is no single right answer — there are better and worse ARIA choices, and your reasoning is part of the work.
- **Mini-projects** are the largest synthesis. They take a full day or more and ship something you would put in your portfolio.

You can skip the challenge and still pass Week 5. But the challenge is where ARIA stops feeling like a checklist and starts feeling like a vocabulary you read fluently. The mini-project takes the same shape; the challenge is the lower-stakes rehearsal.

## Workflow

- Block out a single 2-hour focused session per challenge.
- Disable distractions. Open the Authoring Practices reference for the pattern, your editor, and DevTools — including the Accessibility panel.
- Treat it like a real brief: keyboard-only operation, screen-reader testing, axe-clean. The reviewer is the user who will navigate your portfolio with VoiceOver next year.
- Cite a WCAG Success Criterion in your NOTES.md for each accessibility decision you would defend in a code review. The number, not just "accessibility."
